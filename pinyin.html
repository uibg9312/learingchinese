Pinyin Cheat Sheet

Pinyin Approximate sound (based on American English)
aLike a in mama
aiLike eye (example: Shanghai)
aoLike ow in cow (example: Chairman Mao)
anPinyin a + n
angPinyin an + g
oNo equivalent (when o is preceded by a consonant, use the wo sound [example: bwo, pwo, mwo, fwo]; the o and the uo sounds are very similar)
ongPinyin ou + ng
ouLike oh in "oh no!" or the English letter O
eLike uh in duh or u in up
eiLike ay in bay or the English letter A (example: Beijing)
enLike un in fun
engPinyin e + ng (like ung in fungus)
erNo equivalent, but be sure to curl your tongue up near the top front of your mouth and create a vibration in the front of your mouth while making an 'r' sound
iLike ee in bee or the English letter E
zi, ci, siNo 'i' equivalent, but the sound is similar to the i in zit
zhi, chi, shi, riIn zhi, chi, shi and ri, like the ur in church (for ri, add the 'r' sound described for er)
zhiLike jur in jury
chiLike chur in church
shiLike shir in shirt
riLike r + ur sound (see er and zhi/chi/shi)
iaThe ee pinyin i + pinyin a (use the ee pinyin i for all i that follow below)
iaoPinyin i + pinyin ao
iePinyin i + eh as in education
iuPinyin i + pinyin ou (sounds like the English letters E + O strung together)
ianSounds like the English letters E + N strung together in succession
iangPinyin i + pinyin ang
inNo equivalent, but similar to a cross between seen and sin
ingLike ing in sing
iongPinyin i + pinyin ong
uLike the oo in too
uaPinyin u + pinyin a
uoPinyin u + pinyin o (or wo)
uiPinyin u + pinyin ei (example: Guilin); when following a consonant, ui sounds like way
uaiPinyin u + pinyin ai
uanPinyin u + pinyin an (like the Spanish name 'Juan')
unNo equivalent, but similar to a cross between win and woon (often sounds like 'wun')
uangPinyin u + pinyin ang
üNo equivalent, but be sure to round your lips tightly as if whistling, then say 'ee'
üePinyin ü + eh as in education
üanPinyin ü + the en in when (written as uan after j, q, x, y)
ünNo equivalent, but similar to a cross between win and wean, with rounded lips (like ü + n)
zLike ds in kids
cLike ts in cats
sLike s in say
jSounds very similar to the English letter J (but softer, less voiced)
qNo equivalent, but similar to ch except w/ your tongue flat against the roof of your mouth, behind teeth
xNo equivalent, but similar to sh except w/ your tongue flat against the roof of your mouth, behind teeth
y (as initial)Like y in yes
w (as initial)Like w in we
yanRhymes with 'ten' (but starts with 'y' sound)
yeLike ye in yes
wenNo equivalent, but similar to the English word when
yuanSounds like the English letters U + N strung together in succession (with initial 'y' sound)

Additional notes

  • When using pinyin to type Chinese characters on a computer or cell phone, nü and lü (e.g., used for "woman” 女 and “green” 绿, respectively), are typed into the keyboard as nv and lv.
  • Even though the following pinyin combinations use the üan sound, juan, quan, xuan and yuan are written with a standard u, not with the umlaut (ü).
  • * Denotes that the vowel sound has a variation within the pinyin system (for example the i in zi/ci/si vs ni; yan vs ban; ye vs de; yuan vs duan; etc.)
